SECTION 2 — THE MODEL (4:00 to 5:30)
================================================================================

[SCROLL TO §3 — "Three Explainable Components"]

"Three components. Every number traces to one of these.

[POINT TO FORMULA BLOCK]

Forecast Occupancy equals on-the-books occupancy, plus an empirically-derived
pickup rate, plus an event weight — capped at 100%. Forecast RevPAR is
forecast occupancy times current ADR.

OTB occupancy is ground truth. Not a prediction. Already booked.

Pickup rate is the forecast's real work — how much more will book between now
and arrival. I derived this empirically from the 26 snapshots in the source
data. I'll show you that code in a minute.

Event weight is the lift events add on top. I normalize by room count — a
50,000-person conference means something very different to a 238-room hotel
versus a 3,000-room hotel. I capped it at 15 occupancy points so no single
event can produce an implausible forecast.

Why not ML? Two reasons. Data volume — 4,732 rows, 2 hotels, 26 snapshots.
Not enough. And stakeholders have to be able to ask 'why is July 11 at 100%?'
and get a traceable answer. A transparent formula wins every time in this
data environment."

SECTION 3 — DATA AUDIT: "ONE VERSION OF THE TRUTH"  (5:30 to 9:00)
★ HUAN TRAN'S MOMENT — DO NOT RUSH THIS ★

[SWITCH TO: 1b_data_patch.py — scroll to aggregate_events function]

"Another cover letter question asked about reconciling two datasets into one
version of the truth. This is exactly what happened here.

I started by naively joining OTB with events on market and date. Row count
exploded from 4,732 to 28,276. A 6x fan-out. The cause — multiple events can
hit the same market on the same date, and a naive join duplicates OTB rows
for each event.

If I'd let that through, I would have systematically overstated the event
impact on revenue. The forecast would have been confidently wrong.

[POINT TO aggregate_events FUNCTION]

The fix is this function. Before joining, I aggregate events down to one row
per market-date — summing visitors, counting events, keeping the top category.
That collapses 7,197 event records to 841 day-summaries. Then the join is
clean, one-to-one, row count preserved.

[SCROLL DOWN TO fix_occupancy_outliers]

The second finding was this. 32 rows in hotel_b had occupancy above 100%. My
first instinct was data error, but when I looked at the pattern — it was all
clustered on April 19, 20, and 21. That's Boston Marathon weekend. What I was
actually seeing was real overbooking in the source data. Negative left-to-sell
values. A physical impossibility in inventory, but a real-world condition in
hotel operations.

For model safety, I capped those at 1.0 occupancy. But I flagged the dates as
Recommendation 3 — because the operational signal is more valuable than the
data-hygiene fix. Ops needs to see this.

[SCROLL TO validate_final or similar]

And for the revenue check — I verified rooms_sold times adr_sold equals
revenue_sold across the whole dataset. Max delta was $1.36. Within $1.50
tolerance. Rounding, not a defect.

Those three checks ran as the /reconcile-data skill before any forecast code
executed. That's what I mean when I say the audit is the work, not an

SECTION 4 — PICKUP DERIVATION  (9:00 to 11:00)
================================================================================

[SWITCH TO: 2_forecast_model.py — scroll to compute_pickup_rates]

"Here's the core logic moment. For every future business date, the source
data has multiple snapshots showing how bookings built toward check-in. 26
snapshots for each date at different lead times.

[POINT TO THE INNER LOOP]

What I'm doing here — for every business date, find rooms_sold at lead time
zero, which is check-in. Then for each lead-time bucket — 7, 14, 30, 60, 90
days out — find the closest snapshot. The pickup at that bucket is rooms at
check-in minus rooms at that bucket, divided by total rooms.

Average that across every business date, and you get a hotel-specific pickup
curve. Empirical. Not an external assumption.

[SWITCH TO: chart3_pickup_curve.png — full screen]

And when you plot the two hotels, two distinct personalities emerge.

Boston — steep curve. 56.3% pickup over 90 days. That's 134 incremental rooms.
Demand builds fast. Rate decisions on high-demand dates have to be made early.

Santa Monica — shallow curve. 36.6% over 90 days. 115 rooms. More gradual.
More room for demand stimulation on soft dates.

The shape of these curves is what drives the recommendations."
