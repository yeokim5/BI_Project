**Pattern 1 — Pickup curve asymmetry between leisure and urban conference markets.**
Boston (conference/arts-heavy) picks up 56.3% of remaining rooms over 90 days vs. Santa Monica (leisure) at 36.6%. Urban markets with conference calendars build demand faster and earlier — rate decisions on peak dates must be made at the 60-90 day mark, not 14-30 days out.

**Pattern 2 — Event weight cap behavior.**
At the +15% occupancy cap, events stop differentiating RevPAR outcomes — the marginal driver becomes ADR. Boston's high-visitor events (USENIX, Hans Zimmer) hit the cap frequently. Once capacity is forecast at 100%, the only revenue lever is rate, not volume. This is the trigger for dynamic rate fencing recommendations.

**Pattern 3 — RevPAR spread between market types.**
Boston RevPAR (~$298) was nearly 2x Santa Monica (~$157) despite both being major US markets. The driver is event density and corporate/conference mix, not just ADR. Markets with recurring conference calendars sustain high RevPAR through shoulder seasons (see Boston Sept 9 conference at $427 RevPAR).

**Pattern 4 — Soft window identification.**
Santa Monica's 52–65% occupancy window (Aug 4–Sept 14) was identifiable from the pickup curve combined with low event weight during that period. Soft windows are where demand stimulation ROI is highest — promotions at 45-60 day lead time intercept demand at peak conversion probability.

**Finance communication note:** RevPAR is the cleanest single metric for Finance because it combines both occupancy and rate into one number. When RevPAR is flat despite rising occupancy, it signals rate erosion — a margin concern. When RevPAR rises on flat occupancy, it signals pricing power. Always explain which component is driving RevPAR movement.
